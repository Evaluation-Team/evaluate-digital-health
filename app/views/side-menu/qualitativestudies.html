Qualitative studies

Qualitative studies are often used as you’re developing a product, to work out how to improve it (formative evaluation). They might be part of an iterative development cycle. They can be useful if you have open-ended questions.

While many evaluation approaches concentrate on quantifiable outcomes, there are valuable methods that generate qualitative data. Qualitative approaches can be good for understanding the thoughts and experiences of users of the digital health product, or of other relevant stakeholders. Qualitative methods can give a richer explanation of phenomena. For example, a quantitative approach could describe patterns of disengagement with an app and what demographic factors predict disengagement, but a qualitative approach can describe why users stopped using the app. Qualitative studies may be more subjective than quantitative studies.

While qualitative studies can describe the range of views among participants, they are poor at saying how many participants hold different views. Results from qualitative studies are hard to generalise to other participants.

The number of participants needed for a qualitative study can be much lower than for other evaluation methods. However, the amount of information you get from each participant can be much higher, so usually more time is spent with each participant.

Mixed methods evaluations combine quantitative and qualitative methods. For example, you could collect data on everyone using a product in a before/after study, while also interviewing a subset of users in more depth.

Qualitative research may focus on giving a voice to particular users who might otherwise be overlooked in evaluations.

 

Interview studies

A common approach is a semi-structured interview. Interviews are based on a question guide, but the conversation is allowed to develop. Open-ended questions are used to encourage the participant to share their thoughts.

The interview will usually be recorded and transcribed. It can then be analysed. One common method is thematic analysis.

 

Focus group studies

Instead of talking to participants individually, you could host focus groups with several participants. This way you can hear from multiple people at once, and the conversation between participants can spark further insights that you cannot get in an interview. On the other hand, each person will probably speak less and some people might not want to discuss some topics with a wider audience.

Focus group data is usually analysed in a similar way to interviews.

 

Observational studies or ethnographies

Observational studies can be quantitative or qualitative. In qualitative studies, you could supplement observations with interviews about participants’ perspectives on what was observed. In an ethnography, the researcher seeks to place themself in the context under study. They observe participants’ behaviour and try to understand it from the participants’ own point of view.

 

Other sources of qualitative data

Some sources of qualitative data are routinely collected. For example, app stores usually save reviews submitted by users. Feedback from users of a health care service may be collected. This qualitative data can be analysed, although there may be biases in terms of who chooses to give feedback.
