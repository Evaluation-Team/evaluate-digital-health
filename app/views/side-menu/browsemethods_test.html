Descriptive studies

Understand how things are going (without comparing to something else)

Use them when:

  • your product is in use, to check it works and has no unintended consequences (post-implementation or ex-post evaluation)
  • your product is about to be in use and can be tested with a pilot group to see how it works in practice

They answer questions like:

  • How many people are using your product?
  • How many people remain engaged with the product?
  • How often do they use it?
  • Do users like the product?
  • Do users do well on the product?

Methods include:

Audit

Service evaluation

Analysis of usage data

 

Comparative studies

See whether your digital health product works

Use them when:

  • you want to know how effective your product is, for example, in comparison to not using your product or to using another product
  • you are launching your product, or soon after (summative evaluation)

They answer questions like:

  • What is the difference in health outcomes between people using your product now and how people did before your product was available?
  • What is the difference between people using your product and people using a rival product?
  • What is the difference between people using two versions of your product?

Methods include:

Before/after study

Randomised control trial

A/B testing

 

Qualitative studies

Understand how people feel about your product

Use them when:

  • you want a more in-depth understanding of users’ thoughts and experiences of your product
  • you’re developing your product, to work out how to make it better (formative or iterative evaluation)

They answer questions like:

  • What was users’ overall experience of the product?
  • How did users feel during their journey through the product?
  • What do users want to see in a new version of the product

Methods include:

Interviews

Focus groups

Ethnographic studies
