Hold a workshop to model how your product works

Running a workshop to model how your product will work is useful in the earlier stages of project development. The aim is to produce a living document that can be iterated as your digital health product changes. If you have already developed a model, it’s still useful to revisit this when planning your evaluation.

Creating your model in a workshop helps your team understand:

  • what the project aims are
  • how you will achieve your project aims
  • what evidence you need to prove you have achieved your aims

Organise the workshop

Decide who needs to be involved in your workshop. You should involve key members of your team, who will bring different expertise and perspectives.

Arrange a time when you can meet face to face. 1 to 2 hours is a good workshop duration.

Make sure you have the materials you need. You’ll need sticky notes and pens. You can also print out the templates in the download below and stick them on the walls.

Familiarise yourself with the materials

We’ve created a template for the workshop which can be downloaded and printed. Take time to look at the workshop materials and understand them.

Introduce the activity

Once your team is in the room, explain the purpose of creating a model to show your product is expected to work. Summarise what you will do in the workshop.

Stick up headings

Stick up the headings from the template on the wall so that they resemble the following diagram. You can also stick up the example model to provide inspiration.

Fill out sections

Use the headings and the questions to guide you in completing your model. Put your answers on sticky notes so they can be moved around easily.

You should approach the sections in roughly the following order. Consider the hypothesis, assumptions, context and barriers. Then think about the start and end points in how your product works: the outcomes and the functions. Once you have recorded these, consider how the functions lead to the responses and outcomes. Refer back to the hypothesis, assumptions, context and barriers when thinking about this.

  • hypothesis
  • assumptions
  • context
  • barriers
  • outcomes
  • functions
  • responses

Record your model

Once you are happy with your model, it can be helpful to photograph it to capture the visual representation. Type up your model and share it with your team. Keep it in an editable format like an excel sheet.

Use your model

Use your model to help design your evaluation. What evidence do you need to show that your product is working as intended? How will you measure this?
