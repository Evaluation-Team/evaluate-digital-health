Descriptive methods

Use a descriptive study if you have a digital health product that is in use, or about to be in use, and you want to evaluate how it works. These types of studies describe what is going on. They can give you descriptive statistics (what proportion, how many, how much), or investigate correlations: relationships between variables.

Descriptive studies are weaker at providing evidence of cause and effect. They can’t tell you about counterfactuals: what would have happened if you had done something else.

Descriptive studies are often simple. For digital products they can be quick and cheap if you can use data that is already being collected. Sometimes you will want to collect additional data specifically for the evaluation. This may cost more.

Most descriptive studies use quantitative methods, but some are qualitative, or incorporate qualitative methods.

Examples of descriptive methods:

 

Audit

An audit generally involves examining routinely-collected data to assess whether you are achieving your goals. It is a common approach used in healthcare, where a distinction is made between audits, research and service evaluations. Similar types of studies in other areas may use other names.

Audits are a sensible way to check the performance of an ongoing service. They can identify problems or areas for improvement. However, they cannot tell you about how alternatives would have performed.

Audits are often fairly easy to carry out. They can be set up to run on a regular basis.

 

User surveys

You can carry out a survey asking users of your digital product about their experience of using it. How you do this will depend on the nature of your product or service. You might be able to send questions through the product, for example, through an app. You might be able to send questions separately, for example, through a list of contact email addresses. User surveys may have quantitative and qualitative components.

 

Analyses of log data

Most digital products will record data about their use, such as information on downloads and when people last opened the tool. This can tell you about the success of marketing campaigns, or about users’ engagement with the tool. You may have more detailed information about user journeys through the product or what sections they choose to use.

Users might input data into the digital product, or it may automatically collect data. For example, an app promoting physical activity may collect motion sensor data from a smartphone.

If data is readily available, this can allow a quick and cheap evaluation. However, this approach has limitations. Data that is automatically collected may not answer the questions you have, and it cannot tell you about users who have disengaged from the product.
